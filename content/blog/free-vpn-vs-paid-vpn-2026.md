Free VPNs cost you something. It's just not money.

That statement confuses people. How can a free VPN make money? The answer is almost always: they sell your data, show ads, or limit the service so much that upgrading becomes inevitable. Sometimes all three.

But not all free VPNs are traps. And not all paid VPNs are worth the price. This guide cuts through the noise and tells you exactly what you get and what you give up with each model.

## Why Free VPNs Exist

No legitimate business runs a service at zero cost forever. Free VPNs survive through one of these models:

1. Monetizing your data: Logs are collected, anonymized (or not), and sold to advertisers, data brokers, or researchers. Your browsing patterns become inventory.
2. Showing ads: Every time you connect, ads appear. The longer you stay connected, the more impressions. That's revenue for the VPN provider.
3. Upsells: The free tier is deliberately crippled. Slow speeds. Limited data. Few server choices. After a week of frustration, you upgrade to paid.
4. Bundled services: A free VPN is included with an antivirus suite or file storage service. The VPN itself is a loss leader to lock you into the ecosystem.

The fourth model is the most honest. The first three? You're the product.

## The Specific Risks of Unknown Free VPNs

If you use a free VPN from a provider you've never heard of, ask yourself: who's funding the servers? Who's paying the staff? 

The answer usually involves one or more of these:

**Malware bundling.** Some free VPNs contain malware, either intentionally or through sloppy development. A researcher at Stanford found that 80 percent of free VPN apps on the Google Play Store violated their own privacy policies or contained malicious code. That was in 2016. The situation hasn't improved.

**DNS leaks.** A DNS leak means your ISP still sees which websites you visit, even though you're using a VPN. A free VPN might not properly tunnel DNS requests. Result: your privacy goal is defeated, and you never knew.

**Data selling without permission.** Logs are kept "for security" and then sold to the highest bidder. Marketing firms love VPN user data because it tells them exactly where you browse without using any device-tracking cookies.

**Bandwidth throttling to the point of uselessness.** Free tier gets 50 MB per day. That's about one YouTube video or a few web pages. It's not an accident. It's designed to make you pay.

## The Legitimate Free Options (Yes, They Exist)

Not all free VPNs are scams. A few are genuinely privacy-respecting:

### Proton VPN (Free Tier)

Proton VPN is backed by Proton Mail, a Swiss company known for encryption and privacy. The free tier offers:
- Unlimited data (truly unlimited, not a gimmick)
- Connection to one of three countries (Netherlands, Japan, USA)
- No ads
- No data logging

The catch: you're limited to three servers. The speed can be slower during peak hours. But the privacy is solid. If you check your email once a week on a coffee shop WiFi, Proton VPN free is enough.

Cost: Free. Paid tier (3 servers and faster speeds) costs about 5 euros per month.

### Windscribe (Free Tier)

Windscribe gives you 10 GB of data per month for free, with access to servers in 10 countries. That's enough for moderate use.

- Decent privacy policy (logs are deleted after 15 minutes)
- Multiple server locations
- Works with torrenting (though they ask you not to on the free tier)

The catch: 10 GB is generous but not unlimited. And support for free users is minimal.

Cost: Free (10 GB/month). Paid ($3.36/month for unlimited).

### Tor Browser (Actually Free and Actually Secure)

Tor isn't technically a VPN, but it works better for anonymity. It routes your traffic through multiple servers operated by volunteers, making it nearly impossible to trace. It's slower than any VPN, but the privacy is unmatched.

Cost: Completely free. Maintained by the Tor Project, a nonprofit.

## What Paid VPNs Actually Offer

Paid VPNs aren't just free VPNs with ads removed. They offer real differences:

**More servers.** ProtonVPN paid has servers in 60+ countries. ExpressVPN claims 3000+ servers worldwide. More servers mean faster connections because traffic is distributed. It also means you can appear to be in more countries if you need to watch region-locked content.

**Faster speeds.** Free VPNs prioritize keeping costs low. Paid VPNs invest in better infrastructure. Same website loads in 2 seconds instead of 10.

**Kill switch.** If your VPN connection drops, a kill switch instantly disconnects your internet. You're never exposed with your real IP address. Most free VPNs don't have this.

**Split tunneling.** Route some traffic through the VPN (banking) and some directly (streaming). Paid VPNs let you choose per app. Free VPNs don't.

**Customer support.** Free VPNs have none. Paid VPNs have live chat or email support, often 24/7.

**No data limits.** Truly unlimited use. Download as much as you want.

## The Price Breakdown

A good paid VPN costs between 2 and 5 euros per month if you pay annually. Monthly subscriptions are 8-15 euros.

That breaks down roughly as:
- Server maintenance: costs the provider 20-50 euros per month per server
- Bandwidth: costs 2-10 euros per terabyte depending on provider and region
- Staff and support: maybe 2-3 euros per 100 customers per month
- Marketing and overhead: 1-2 euros per customer per month

A provider with 1 million customers and 200 servers can profitably run at 3-4 euros per customer per month. That's why that's the price range you see.

## When Free VPN is Enough

- **Occasional coffee shop use.** You're just checking email and the news. No sensitive data. Proton VPN free tier is fine.
- **Already trust the provider.** Your antivirus suite includes a VPN from a known company. Norton, Kaspersky, Bitdefender all include VPNs with their paid antivirus. Those are worth using because the provider's reputation is already on the line.
- **Torrenting on a public WiFi once.** Just connecting once to a Proton VPN free server is safer than nothing.

## When You Need Paid

- **Daily use.** If you're connecting every day, the better speeds and more server choices of paid are worth the money.
- **Torrenting or P2P.** Paid VPNs are more likely to stand up to legal pressure to log your data. Most free VPNs don't have the legal resources to fight.
- **Streaming.** Free VPNs are routinely blocked by Netflix, Disney, and others. Paid VPNs invest in staying unblocked.
- **Work from home.** Your company's security team will probably ask you not to use a free VPN. Paid VPNs from known providers are usually approved.

## The One Rule

Never use a free VPN whose business model you can't identify.

If you can't explain how the provider makes money, don't use it. Ask the provider directly in their FAQ or contact form. A legitimate free VPN will have a clear answer. An answer like "we're funded by investors" is fine (that usually means ad-supported or upsell-focused, but at least it's honest). An answer like "we don't know yet" or no answer at all? Move on.

## The Summary

Free VPNs can work for light use from trusted providers (Proton, Windscribe). Paid VPNs ($3-5/month) are worth the money if you use a VPN regularly or need it for anything sensitive. Never use a free VPN from a company you've never heard of. Speed, privacy, and peace of mind are worth the cost.
