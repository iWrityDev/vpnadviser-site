The question sounds simple. The answer is not.

Free VPNs exist. Paid VPNs exist. They serve different use cases. And your decision should depend not on which is cheaper, but on whether the free option actually delivers privacy or just the illusion of it.

## The Core Difference

A free VPN survives by monetizing something. Usually it's you.

A paid VPN survives by charging you directly.

That sounds cynical, but it's the most important distinction. When a business doesn't charge for a product, the product is often the user's attention, data, or both. A VPN that claims to be free but doesn't explain its business model is a red flag. How are they paying for servers? Staff? Licenses? The answer matters.

## What Free VPNs Actually Cost You

The monetary cost is zero. The privacy cost is not.

### Data Monetization

Some free VPN providers log your browsing history and sell it to the highest bidder. Data brokers pay premium rates for VPN user data because it's geographically tagged, time-stamped, and shows exactly which websites were visited. That's more valuable than most cookies because it can't be easily blocked. Your browsing history becomes inventory.

### Speed Bottlenecks

Free VPN providers deliberately slow down free connections to incentivize upgrades. It's not a limitation of the technology. It's a business model. If free users could stream 4K video and download torrents at 100 Mbps, no one would ever pay.

Result: a free VPN that works is often barely usable.

### Limited Servers

Free users get access to maybe 5-10 servers. Paid tiers unlock 60+. The difference matters for two reasons. First, fewer servers means more users per server, which slows everyone down. Second, if you need to appear in a specific country (accessing region-locked content, for example), a limited server list might not have that country.

### No Kill Switch

If your VPN connection drops, do you want to keep browsing with your real IP exposed? Of course not. A kill switch automatically cuts your internet if the VPN connection dies. Most free VPNs don't have this. Many paid VPNs do.

## What Paid VPNs Deliver

For 3-5 euros per month (annual pricing), you get:

**More servers.** 60+ countries instead of 5-10. This means faster speeds (load is distributed) and more options if you need a specific location.

**Faster speeds.** Paid VPN providers invest in better infrastructure. It's not magic, it's infrastructure. They have more servers, better routing, and they don't deliberately bottleneck connections.

**Kill switch and split tunneling.** Kill switch cuts internet if the VPN drops. Split tunneling lets you route some apps through the VPN and others directly (so you can use a VPN for banking and normal internet for streaming).

**Real customer support.** Live chat. Email. Actual humans if something breaks.

**No data selling.** Legitimate paid VPN providers have a business model that doesn't require monetizing your data. You're the customer, not the product.

**Logging policy transparency.** Reputable paid VPNs publish audits proving they don't keep logs. Free VPNs rarely bother.

## The Specific Risks of Unverified Free VPNs

If a free VPN provider is not well-known, assume one of these is true:

1. They're selling your data and hiding it under vague privacy language.
2. They're making money through forced ads or aggressive upsells.
3. They're poorly maintained and your data is at risk through simple negligence.
4. They've bundled malware.

Studies have found that many free VPN apps on app stores contain malware or violate their own privacy policies. It's not paranoia. It's documented.

## The Exception: Legitimate Free VPNs

A handful of free VPNs are genuinely trustworthy because their business model is transparent:

**Proton VPN (Free Tier):** Unlimited data, three server locations, no logging, no ads. Proton Mail (their parent company) has built a reputation for privacy. The free tier is a loss leader, but it's honest.

**Windscribe (Free Tier):** 10 GB per month, 10 countries, decent privacy policy. Transparent about how they make money (upgrades to paid plans). Not unlimited, but legit.

Neither of these is as good as their paid tier. Both are actually usable without paying.

## When Free is Enough

- **One-time use.** You're at an airport, need to check email securely once, then you're done. Free is fine.
- **Light browsing.** Checking news on a coffee shop WiFi. No sensitive data. Free works.
- **You already trust the provider.** Your antivirus includes a free VPN from a known company. That's usually safe.

## When Paid is Necessary

- **Daily use.** If you're connecting every day, paid VPN's better speeds and more servers are worth the cost.
- **Torrenting or downloading files.** Paid VPN providers are more likely to defend your privacy legally. Free VPNs won't spend money on lawyers.
- **Streaming.** Free VPNs are blocked by Netflix, Disney, and others. Paid VPNs constantly invest in staying unblocked.
- **Work from home.** Your employer's security team will request you use a paid VPN from a reputable provider. They can't guarantee the privacy practices of unknown free services.
- **Any sensitive activity.** Banking, medical info, personal documents. Use a paid VPN from a provider you've verified.

## The Price Reality

A good paid VPN costs 3-5 euros per month if you pay annually. Monthly plans cost more, around 8-15 euros.

Why that range? The economics are straightforward.

- Servers cost the provider 20-50 euros per month each to operate and maintain.
- Bandwidth costs 2-10 euros per terabyte.
- Staff and support is maybe 2-3 euros per 100 customers monthly.
- Marketing and overhead is 1-2 euros per customer monthly.

A provider with proper infrastructure, good privacy practices, and honest business practices needs at least 3-4 euros per customer monthly to break even. That's why you don't see 99-cent VPNs from legitimate companies. If a VPN costs almost nothing, they're cutting corners somewhere.

## How to Decide

Ask yourself three questions:

1. **Do I know who funds this VPN?** Can they explain their business model? If not, skip it.
2. **How often will I use it?** Once a month or less? Free might work. Daily? Paid is worth it.
3. **What am I using it for?** Casual browsing? Free is fine. Anything sensitive? Paid, from a known provider.

The difference between free and paid isn't just speed or server count. It's the difference between using a service built to respect your privacy and using one that might be monetizing it. For occasional use from a trusted provider, free works. For regular use or anything sensitive, paid from a reputable company is the better choice.

The 3-4 euros per month is a worthwhile investment in actual privacy, not just the illusion of it.
