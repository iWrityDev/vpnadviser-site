# VPN Jurisdiction: Why Where a VPN Is Based Matters (and When It Doesn't)

VPN companies operate from a physical location, and that location determines which laws they must follow. Understanding jurisdiction is one of the most misunderstood aspects of VPN privacy. Some people believe that a VPN in Panama or Switzerland is inherently safer than one in the US. Others assume jurisdiction doesn't matter at all. The truth is more nuanced.

## What Jurisdiction Actually Means

Jurisdiction is the country where a VPN company is legally registered and conducts business. It is not just a marketing choice. It is a binding legal reality. A company registered in the United States must comply with US law, including subpoenas, National Security Letters (NSLs), and court orders. A company registered in Panama must comply with Panamanian law. This matters because different countries have different rules about data retention, government access, and privacy rights.

A VPN company's jurisdiction determines:
1. What data it is legally required to keep and for how long
2. Whether the government can compel it to install logging software
3. What happens if a court orders the company to hand over user data
4. How transparent the company must be about these demands

## The 5 Eyes, 9 Eyes, and 14 Eyes Alliances

These intelligence-sharing agreements between nations determine how information flows between governments. If your data is in one country and a different country wants it, these alliances may apply.

The 5 Eyes alliance (US, UK, Canada, Australia, New Zealand) share signals intelligence directly. If you are under investigation in the US and your VPN company is in the UK, the agencies can share data without formal legal process.

The 9 Eyes adds France, Netherlands, Norway, and Denmark. The 14 Eyes adds South Korea, Japan, Spain, Sweden, and Italy.

The implication: if your VPN is in a 5 Eyes country, a government in any of the other 4 Eyes countries can potentially access your data without your VPN company even knowing about it. This is why VPN companies based in non-alliance countries are considered "safer" in theory.

## Favorable Jurisdictions

**Panama**
Mullvad's infrastructure is based partly in Panama. Panama has no mandatory data retention laws for internet companies. This means a Panamanian company cannot be forced to keep logs it never created in the first place. A court order demanding user logs gets no response because the logs don't exist. This is the strongest legal position a VPN can be in.

**British Virgin Islands**
NordVPN is based in the BVI. The territory has no mandatory data retention law, similar to Panama. However, the BVI is a British Overseas Territory, which creates some legal ambiguity about whether 5 Eyes intelligence sharing agreements apply. NordVPN has committed to no-log policies and undergone independent audits, which provides some assurance despite the jurisdiction question.

**Switzerland**
ProtonVPN is based in Switzerland. Switzerland has strong privacy laws and is not part of any Eyes alliance. Swiss law offers robust privacy protections for companies. However, Switzerland does cooperate with international law enforcement when facing legitimate requests. The advantage is that Switzerland requires evidence and proper legal process before compelling companies to assist.

**Romania**
Mullvad is also registered in Romania. Romania is an EU country, which means data protection rules are stricter than in the US. The country is not part of 5 Eyes. However, Romania is part of international law enforcement treaties and will cooperate with requests.

## The Critical Question: Does Jurisdiction Actually Matter More Than No-Log Audits?

Here is where many VPN discussions get this backwards: jurisdiction is secondary to audit verification.

A VPN company in a favorable jurisdiction (Panama, BVI, Switzerland) with NO independent audit is less trustworthy than a VPN in a unfavorable jurisdiction (US, UK) that has been independently audited and verified to have no logs.

Why? Because a favorable jurisdiction is only useful if the company actually follows its no-log policy. If a company claims to keep no logs but has never been audited, you have to take it on faith. If a company has been audited by a third party that confirmed the absence of logging infrastructure, you have evidence.

A real example: Mullvad (Panama, Romania, Sweden) has undergone independent security audits that verified the absence of identifying information. The audit found Mullvad could not identify users even if compelled to do so. This is stronger than any other VPN's claims about jurisdiction.

NordVPN (BVI) has undergone audits, but the audits have not been as comprehensive as Mullvad's. The favorable jurisdiction helps, but the audit is where the real confidence comes from.

ExpressVPN (BVI) claims no logs but has never undergone a verified third-party audit. The favorable jurisdiction is less meaningful without proof.

## Why It Matters Less Than You Think

Jurisdiction becomes less relevant if a government wants your data badly enough. A National Security Letter in the US can compel a company to install spyware on users' connections without any court review. A VPN in Switzerland cannot prevent a Swiss court order, and a VPN in Panama cannot prevent an international extradition request for its owner.

Jurisdiction protects against casual requests and automated data harvesting. It does not protect against targeted, high-stakes surveillance.

What actually protects you:
1. A VPN that cannot produce identifying data because it never collected it in the first place (no-log architecture)
2. Verification via independent audit that the no-log claim is real
3. A company that publishes transparency reports showing government requests and how it responded
4. A company owner who refuses to compromise even under legal pressure

## The Real Question to Ask

Instead of obsessing over whether your VPN is in Panama or Switzerland, ask these questions:

1. Has this VPN been independently audited for no-logs?
2. Does the audit report show that the company has no infrastructure to log users?
3. How does the company respond to government requests? (Check their transparency reports)
4. Has the company ever been compromised or caught logging despite claims?

A VPN in the US that has been audited and verified to have no logs is safer than a VPN in Panama that claims no logs but has never been verified.

A VPN in Switzerland with a published transparency report showing it received government requests and handed over zero user data is more trustworthy than speculation about jurisdiction.

## The Bottom Line

Jurisdiction matters, but audit verification and transparency matter more. A favorable jurisdiction is a starting point for privacy. It is not a guarantee. Mullvad's strength comes from both its favorable jurisdiction (multiple countries) and its verified no-log architecture (independent audits). ProtonVPN's strength comes from Switzerland's privacy laws plus its published transparency reports.

When choosing a VPN, prioritize the ones with audited no-log claims and transparency reports over the ones that are simply based in a "good" country. Jurisdiction is one factor in a larger security model, not the whole model.
